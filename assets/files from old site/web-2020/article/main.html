3-pointer Robots

By Beth Sweet

The State News

Tytus Gerrish · FIRST Robotics

Team 1504 GEOmotion from Grand Ledge and Okemos High Schools  “dumps” 7-inch diameter balls into a 1-point Goal during the Detroit Regional at Wayne State University

 

On March 16-18 and 23-25, GEOmotion competed at two regionals and put the Lansing area on the map.

 

GEOmotion is a FIRST (For Inspiration and Recognition of Science and Technology) robotics team that is comprised of students from Grand Ledge and Okemos High Schools and mentors who are parents, as well as Michigan State University students. 

 

This year, the team competed at the Detroit Regional at Wayne State University and at the Wisconsin Regional in Milwaukee, Wisc.  At the Detroit competition, the team made it to the quarterfinals of the tournament and in Milwaukee, they went one step further and got to the semifinals. 

 

“Seeing the robot that the kids put so much time into perform at such high caliber was phenomenal. You could see the excitement in the kids' eyes and you could tell that our jobs as mentors, to inspire the kids, had just happened,” says team mentor and MSU junior Pat Mullen. 

 

“We did well because we were a simplistic robot and complimented the other teams very well,” said Grand Ledge High School junior Adam DiPietro.

 

The competitions in which the team participated were just the end of a season that began back in January.  On January 7, the team attended a “kick-off” event where they found out what game they had to create a robot to play.

 

The 2006 game was basketball; robot-style.  Three red robots played against three blue robots in back-and-forth periods of offense.  While on offense, each team would try to shoot and score as many three- and one-point baskets as possible.  At the end of the game, all three robots could drive onto a ramp for bonus points. 

 

Faced with this challenge and a set of size and weight regulations, the team began to design a robot to play this game.  They were supplied with several items that are used in real-life engineering applications, including motors and gearboxes, a color-sensing camera and the software for the programming language C.

 

“The motors and gearboxes that we used for the roller system were originally designed for use in Power Wheels, but we modified ours to produce more speed,” says Mullen. 

 

An essential for any team trying to shoot was to use the CMUcam2.  This device is a high-tech, mini-camera.  It is programmed to look around and can “see” the colors blue, red and green.  In the game, the camera finding its color was how the robot knew what direction to shoot in. 

 

The robot was controlled by drivers using joysticks for most of the match.  These joysticks had to be programmed to make the robot do what it needed to do, however.  This was the job of the team’s programmer.  Using Easy C, a new piece of programming software, GEOmotion’s programmer made the connection between these two essential pieces.  Easy C is an easy to use program because instead of the typical combinations of letters, numbers and symbols, it uses pictures, similarly to a computer game. 

 

During the six weeks that the team had to design and build their 130 pound robot, they truly became a team.

 

“We all bonded like a tight family.  It’s nice to work with people from another school, there’s a whole new group of people,” says DiPietro. 

 

Parent-turned-mentor Mike Murphy says that being a parent on the team has had its highs and lows.

 

“I am excited about what FIRST is all about, and what it brings to the kids. I am frustrated at the lack of awareness of the program,” he says.

 

Though GEOmotion has finished their season this year they are already creating plans for next year.

 

“Next year we hope to have acquired not only more money through sponsors, but also more manual help.  This should come through the addition of machinists and engineers, which we are severely lacking at this moment.  With more professional help, hopefully the kids will gain more of an understanding of engineering; more than what a few business majors and a journalism major can bring to the table,” says Mullen.
